There's a moment, right when something finally works, when your brain pays you. The test goes green. The deploy lands clean. The idea you had in your head shows up on screen. That payment has a chemical name: dopamine. And for years it was expensive.

It cost something. You had to fight the refactor you'd been putting off for months "for lack of time". You had to sit down a whole afternoon to ship the idea you weren't sure about. The hit came at the end, after the work — and that's why it meant something.

AI changed the price. Now the goal is one call away. The refactor you didn't have time for, you have in twenty minutes. The idea you weren't sure you'd ship in time ships tonight. It's wonderful.

And that's exactly the problem.

## DaaS: Dopamine as a Service

When the rush of finishing becomes available on demand, you start wanting it more often. And what you optimise for, almost without noticing, stops being the result. It becomes the hit.

It's the same mechanic as any infinite feed. You don't stay for the last video — you stay for the next one. Same here. You don't ship because the feature is ready. You ship because "done" feels good, and the next dose is one prompt away.

Code stops being a means. It becomes the lever you pull to make the machine release the reward.

## When you lose control

I've been on projects where you can feel this. It doesn't start with a bad decision. It starts with a good feeling: *"this is going so fast."*

The need to have it as soon as possible stops being external pressure and turns into your own desire. You want to see the thing working now, because seeing it work is the prize. So you accept the first output that compiles. You don't fully read the PR. You don't argue the design decision the agent made for you, because arguing it delays the hit.

And one day the project no longer fits in your head. Nobody decided it should stop fitting. Each "come on, this quick" was just a bit of control you traded for a dose. You never saw the sum coming, because every single dose looked free.

The cheap thing was never the code. The cheap thing is the feeling of progress.

## The caveat

Dopamine isn't the enemy. It's what makes you enjoy building, and work without that reward becomes unbearable. The goal isn't to stop feeling it.

The goal is not to let it decide for you. To keep the hit coming *after* judgment, not instead of it. To ship because it's right, and to keep finishing hard enough that it still means something.

## The line

Next time you're about to accept something blindly, ask yourself: am I doing this because it's right, or because I want the rush of having finished it?

AI is an extraordinary machine for reaching the goal. Just make sure you're still the one choosing which goal you run toward.
